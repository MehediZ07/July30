Before July · কোটা প্রথা

A hiring quota, revived by a court

The spark was procedural. The fire was decades in the making.

Since 1972, Bangladesh had reserved a large share of civil‑service jobs for specific groups, most notably a 30% quota for children and grandchildren of veterans of the 1971 Liberation War. In a country with chronic graduate unemployment, students had challenged the system before — in 2013 and again in 2018, when mass protests pushed the government to scrap it almost entirely.

On 5 June 2024, the High Court reinstated the 2018 quota system, ruling the government's cancellation of it invalid. To students who had spent years preparing for a shrinking pool of merit‑based posts, it read as the reversal of a hard‑won reform — and reopened a grievance that had never really closed.

The 36 days · জুলাই অভ্যুত্থানের সময়রেখা

From blockade to uprising

A movement that began with sit‑ins on university lawns and ended with a prime minister's helicopter leaving the capital.

1–8 JULY
Bangla Blockadeবাংলা ব্লকেড
Students under the new banner Students Against Discrimination block major roads, rail lines and highways nationwide. The protest is peaceful, campus‑based, and framed narrowly around quota reform: "Quota na medha, medha medha" — quota or merit, merit, merit.
10–14 JULY
A pause, a reprieve, a provocation
The Appellate Division suspends the quota reinstatement for four weeks, easing tension briefly. Then, at a press conference on 14 July, Sheikh Hasina responds to a question about the quota with a remark widely heard as comparing protesters to "Razakars" — wartime collaborators, one of the gravest insults in Bangladeshi political language. Campuses erupt within hours.
Turning point
15–16 JULY
The first bloodপ্রথম রক্ত
On 15 July, activists of the ruling party's student wing, Bangladesh Chhatra League, attack protesters at Dhaka University. The next day, Abu Sayed, a student in Rangpur, is shot dead by police as he stands with arms spread, unarmed, in front of advancing officers — captured on video that spreads nationwide within hours. Five more protesters are killed the same day. The government shuts all schools and universities indefinitely.
Abu Sayed killed
18–19 JULY
Curfew, army, blackout
Mugdho, a young volunteer distributing water and biscuits to protesters, is shot dead in Dhaka on 18 July — his death, like Abu Sayed's, becomes a symbol of the movement. That evening the government cuts mobile internet nationwide; broadband follows within a day. A nationwide curfew is declared and the army is deployed. 19 July becomes the single deadliest day of the month, with dozens of reported deaths as security forces and ruling‑party activists confront protesters in multiple cities.
Total blackout begins
21 JULY
The court finally rules
With the country under curfew, the Appellate Division overhauls the quota system: 93% of civil‑service posts by merit, 5% for freedom fighters' descendants, the remainder split among ethnic minorities, third‑gender and disabled applicants. The original demand is essentially met — but by now the movement is no longer about quotas.
30 JULY
Red, not blackকালো নয়, লাল
The government declares a national day of mourning for those killed. Ruling‑party supporters change their social media profile pictures to black. Across the country, students and ordinary citizens instead turn theirs red — a deliberate, leaderless rejection of official grief, and a demand for accountability rather than ceremony.
3–4 AUGUST
One demand: resignation
At Shaheed Minar on 3 August, coordinator Nahid Islam announces the movement's single remaining demand — the resignation of Sheikh Hasina and her cabinet — and calls for a nationwide non‑cooperation movement from 4 August. That day brings some of the worst violence of the uprising, with well over a hundred deaths reported across the country as clashes spread beyond the capital.
One‑point demand
5 AUGUST
The Long March to Dhakaঢাকা অভিমুখে লংমার্চ
Defying a nationwide curfew, hundreds of thousands converge on the capital. By early afternoon, Sheikh Hasina resigns and leaves the country by military helicopter with her sister, ending fifteen years in power. Crowds enter the prime minister's official residence, Ganabhaban, within hours. An interim government forms days later under Nobel laureate Muhammad Yunus.
Government falls
The blackout · ব্ল্যাকআউট

A country switched off

For nearly ten days, Bangladesh's 170 million people were cut off from the open internet — and from each other.

Mobile data went first, on the evening of 18 July. Broadband followed. Facebook, WhatsApp and other platforms stayed restricted even after connections partially returned, reopening fully only in early August. Hospitals were reportedly barred from disclosing casualty figures; foreign journalists struggled to verify events on the ground.

The UN human rights office later noted that the blackout itself made an accurate death toll impossible to establish — the documented figures below are described by investigators as likely undercounts.

The signature gesture

A red square,
instead of black

When the state called for mourning, ordinary Bangladeshis improvised their own symbol — no leader announced it, no organisation coordinated it. It simply spread, account by account, until red became the color of the uprising.

Afterward · পরবর্তী

A government falls, a country rebuilds

Sheikh Hasina's Awami League had governed Bangladesh for fifteen consecutive years. On 5 August 2024, she resigned and flew to India, where she has remained since; her whereabouts and political future are still disputed inside Bangladesh.

An interim government led by economist and Nobel Peace laureate Muhammad Yunus took office days later, tasked with stabilising the country and preparing for fresh elections. The organisers of the protest movement, largely unknown outside student politics before June 2024, became central figures in the country's transitional politics.

Every year on 16 July — the date Abu Sayed was killed — Bangladesh now observes July Martyrs' Day.
